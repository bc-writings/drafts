    More a visual proof than combinatorial, perhaps:

    52−42+32−22+12=(62)52−42+32−22+12=(62)

    On the left, you have the alternating sum as an inclusion-exclusion of squares: the total sum is the number of coloured cells.

    On the right, you have those L shaped shapes rearranged in the top left of a 6x6 grid. If you think of each cell as a coordinate (𝑥1,𝑥2)(x1,x2) that gives two elements chosen from the set {1,2⋯6}{1,2⋯6}, it's seen that the elements are choosen with 𝑥2>𝑥1x2>x1, what corresponds to a combination (no repetition, and no order).

    Added: The others are well known, but, just for the sake of completeness...

    ∑𝑘=1𝑛(−1)𝑛−𝑘𝑘2=(𝑛+12)=∑𝑘=1𝑛𝑘=(𝑛+1)𝑛2∑k=1n(−1)n−kk2=(n+12)=∑k=1nk=(n+1)n2

    Get rid of the negative signs by putting together the two last terms of the sum, then the two last before these, and so on. The result is the area of a subset of the 𝑛×𝑛n×n square made of 1×11×1 squares composing L-shaped strips: the largest strip is the union of the 𝑛n column and the 𝑛n horizontal line, consecutive strips are separated by similar strips, each strip starts from the vertical axis of coordinates then goes East then goes South and ends at the horizontal axis of coordinates, and I hope that with these indications everybody can picture the result.

    Now, color in black the strips composing the subset one wants to measure and color in white the rest of the rectangle:

    ◻◻◻◻◻◻◻◻◻◻
    ◼◼◼◼◼◼◼◼◼◼
    ◻◻◻◻◼◻◻◻◻◼

    ◻◻◼◻◼◻◻◼◻◼
    ◼◻◼◻◼◼◻◼◻◼

    The resulting rectangle has size 𝑛×(𝑛+1)n×(n+1) and I pretend that it is evenly colored. This will prove the result since its area is 𝑛(𝑛+1)n(n+1).

    To show this, one can pair together sub-rectangles of opposite colors. The first pair is made of the whole line 𝑛+1n+1 (white) and the whole line 𝑛n (black). Erase them both. The resulting rectangle has size 𝑛×(𝑛−1)n×(n−1):

    ◻◻◻◻◼◻◻◻◻◼
    ◼◼◼◻◼◼◼◼◻◼
    ◻◻◼◻◼◻◻◼◻◼
    ◼◻◼◻◼◼◻◼◻◼

    Its last two columns have opposite colors, erase them both. The resulting rectangle has size (𝑛−2)×(𝑛−1)(n−2)×(n−1) and it is the augmented rectangle one would consider to solve the 𝑛−2n−2 case:

    ◻◻◻◻◻◻
    ◼◼◼◼◼◼
    ◻◻◼◻◻◼
    ◼◻◼◼◻◼

    Either continue these erasure steps till the end (where one gets an evenly colored rectangle of size 1×21×2 if 𝑛n is odd and 2×12×1 if 𝑛n is even) or assume that the hypothesis holds at rank 𝑛−2n−2, either way one is done.

    Edit An alternative, maybe simpler, is to peel the 𝑛×(𝑛+1)n×(n+1) rectangle, starting from the top and right sides, one L-shape after the other. For example the first L-shape is the union of the 2𝑛2n elementary squares in the last line and the last column.Then the result follows from the fact that these L-shapes are all evenly colored,

    We can use the very nice picture in the post of Didier Piau (without the added white line on top) and tell a somewhat different story.

    Imagine that 𝑛n is odd, because the picture does. Things hardly change if 𝑛n is even.

    First (and unimportantly) we change the story that led to the colouring. Reverse the addition, so that we are looking at 52−42+32−22+1252−42+32−22+12. If this "algebraic" trick is not combinatorial enough, we could work our way around it, but prefer not to.

    By a 𝑗×𝑗j×j subsquare we mean the 𝑗×𝑗j×j square with bottom left corner the same as the bottom left corner of the original square.

    Colour the full square black. Then colour the (𝑛−1)×(𝑛−1)(n−1)×(n−1) subsquare white, then the (𝑛−2)×(𝑛−2)(n−2)×(n−2) subsquare black, and so on. This inclusion-exclusion process mirrors exactly our alternating sum, and produces the black-white pattern of the picture.

    Next we interpret the upside down and backwards black "L-shaped" figures. We want to end up with (𝑛+12)(n+12).

    Imagine choosing 22 objects from the numbers 11 to 𝑛+1n+1. We have the following possibilities: (i) the biggest number used is 𝑛+1n+1 or 𝑛n; (ii) the biggest number used is 𝑛−1n−1 or 𝑛−2n−2; and so on.

    Look at the largest black L-shaped figure. Its upper right-hand corner counts the choice {𝑛,𝑛+1}{n,n+1}. The remaining horizontal black squares of the big L counts the choices 𝑛−1n−1 choices {𝑘,𝑛+1}{k,n+1} with 𝑘<𝑛k<n. And the remaining vertical black squares of the big L counts the choices (𝑘,𝑛)(k,n) with 𝑘<𝑛k<n.

    In the same way, the next black L-shaped figure counts the choice from the 𝑛+1n+1 numbers, where the largest chosen number is 𝑛−1n−1 or 𝑛−2n−2. And so on.

    You can also increase 𝑛n by one:

    𝐿𝐻𝑆𝑛=𝑛2−𝐿𝐻𝑆𝑛−1=…LHSn=n2−LHSn−1=…
    I had to add the last term 𝑛2n2. However, the signs of all the previous terms were reverted because of the (−1)𝑛(−1)n factor, so I had to add a minus sign in front of 𝐿𝐻𝑆𝑛−1LHSn−1.

    Assuming that the equation holds for 𝑛−1n−1, i.e. that 𝐿𝐻𝑆𝑛−1=𝑅𝐻𝑆𝑛−1LHSn−1=RHSn−1, the displayed formula is

    ⋯=𝑛2−𝑅𝐻𝑆𝑛−1=𝑛2−(𝑛−1)𝑛/2=𝑛(𝑛+1)/2=𝑅𝐻𝑆𝑛⋯=n2−RHSn−1=n2−(n−1)n/2=n(n+1)/2=RHSn
    which you wanted to be proved.
