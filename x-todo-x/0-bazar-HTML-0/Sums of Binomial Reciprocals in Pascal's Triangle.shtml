Sums of Binomial Reciprocals in Pascal's Triangle

A post at the CutTheKnotMath facebook page by Daniel Hardisky brought to my attention the following pattern in Pascal's triangle:

The sums of the reciprocals of the binomial coefficients over successive diagonals

∑k=0∞1Cn+kk=nn−1, n>1.

The sum for n=0 is obviously ∞ and so is for n=1 which is just the harmonic series which is known to diverge to infinity.

It appears that such sums, where the binomial reciprocals appear in the denominator, are still very much a research topic. The problem at hand has several known solutions. I chose the one that appeals to the telescoping property of the series involved.

By definition, 1Cn+kk=n!k!(n+k)!=n!(k+1)(k+2)⋯(k+n).

Invoking the concept of partial fractions,

1(k+1)(k+n)=1n−1(1k+1−1k+n),

which allows us to write

n!(k+1)(k+2)⋯(k+n)=n!n−1(1(k+1)⋯(k+n−1)−1(k+2)⋯(k+n)).

It follows that

∑k=0∞1Cn+kk= n!n−1∑k=0∞(1(k+1)⋯(k+n−1)−1(k+2)⋯(k+n))=n!n−1(∑k=0∞1(k+1)⋯(k+n−1)−∑k=0∞1(k+2)⋯(k+n))=n!n−1(∑k=0∞1(k+1)⋯(k+n−1)−∑k=1∞1(k+1)⋯(k+n−1))=n!n−11(k+1)⋯(k+n−1)∣∣∣k=0=n!n−11(n−1)!=nn−1.

Daniel also observed that there is just one step from nn−1 to the plain reciprocal 1n−1:

∑k=1∞1Cn+kk=1n−1, n>1.

Now, the integer reciprocals are known to combine into ln2 via the Taylor series ln(1+x)=∑n=2(−1)nxn−1n−1:

ln2=1−12+13−+(−1)n1n−1+,

which leads to the following combination:

ln2=∑n=2∞(−1)n∑k=1∞1Cn+kk.
