Another Binomial Identity with Proofs

The purpose of this page is to present two proofs of an identity that involves binomial coefficients. I'll be using a shorter than usual notation (nm) for the binomial coefficient Cnm.

∑k=0n(nk)2=(2nn).

Combinatorial Proof

Recollect that (nk)=(nn−k) and rewrite the required identity as

∑k=0n(nk)(nn−k)=(2nn).

In this form it admits a simple interpretation. It is required to select an n-members committee out of a group of n men and n women. The number of possibilities is (2nn), the right hand side of the identity.

On the other hand, if the number of men in a group of n grownups is k then the number of women is n−k, and all possible variants are expressed by the left hand side of the identity.

Proof with Generating Functions

(1+x)n is the generating function for the binomial coefficients (nk),0≤k≤n. Obviously, (1+x)n(1+x)n=(1+x)2n. On the right, the coefficient by xn is (2nn). On the left, it is ∑nk=0(nk)(nn−k).

In fact, with a little change of the language, we can prove a more general identity:

∑k≥0(nk)(mk)=(n+mn).

(The assumption here is that, for a<b, (ab)=0.)

For a combinatorial argument, the number of men n is not necessarily equal to the number of women m. With generating functions, we use (1+x)n(1+x)m=(1+x)n+m.
