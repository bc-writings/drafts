Counting Fat Sets

We start with the set ℕn={1,2,…,n}. A subset A⊂ℕn is said to be fat if, for every x∈A, x≥|A|, where |A| is the cardinality of, i.e., the number of elements in, A. (Fat sets have been introduced by George Andrews.)

As an example, with n=4, the sets ∅, {1}, {2}, {3}, {4}, {2,3}, {2,4}, {3,4} are fat subsets of ℕn, while the sets {1,2}, {1,3}, {1,4}, {1,2,3}, {1,2,4}, {1,3,4}, {2,3,4}, and {1,2,3,4} are not.

How many fat subsets are there in ℕn? Let's denote that number fn. We shall prove a recursive formula:

The number fn of fat subsets of ℕn satisfies the recurrence

fn+1 = fn + fn−1, n≥2,

with f1=2 and f2=3.

Proof

Let A⊂ℕn is fat. There are two possibilities: n∈A and n∉A. In the latter case, A is a fat subset of ℕn−1 and counts in fn−1. Assume, n∈A. In that case, 1∉A, because, otherwise, we would have |A|≥2>1, making A non-fat. Define B=A∖{n}, and C={x−1: x∈B}. C is then a fat subset of ℕn−2. We see that any fat subset A of ℕn is either a fat subset of ℕn−1 or uniquely corresponds to a fat subset of ℕn−2. The converse is also true, thus proving the recurrence. In addition, A⊂ℕ1 has only two fat subset, ∅ and {1}, while A⊂ℕ2 has three, ∅, {1}, and {2}.

Obviously, {fn} is a "shifted" Fibonacci sequence {Fn}, where F0=F1=1: fn=Fn+2.

There is another way to count the fat subsets of A⊂ℕn.

There is only one way to pick the empty set. There are (n1) ways to select a one-element subset. Since 1 couldn't be included in fat subsets of more than 1 element, there are (n−12) 2-element fat subsets. For a similar reason, 2 is excluded from fat subsets with more than 2 elements so that there are (n−23) 3-element subsets, and so on. The question is where do we stop? This depends on the parity of n:

fn={(n+10)+(n1)+(n−12)+…+((n+1)/2(n+1)/2),(n+10)+(n1)+(n−12)+…+((n+2)/2n/2),if n is oddif n is even
